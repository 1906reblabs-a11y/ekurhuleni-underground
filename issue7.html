The hidden systems of the East Rand

Theme Analysis — Issue 7

Airport Economy

Why this theme matters for the long-term economic story of Ekurhuleni

The theme of the Airport Economy is not a story about aeroplanes. It is a story about how a single piece of infrastructure — O.R. Tambo International Airport — has quietly reorganised the economic geography of an entire metropolitan region. Every logistics warehouse in Boksburg, every freight-forwarding agent in Kempton Park, every cold-chain truck rumbling through Germiston before dawn is, in some sense, a product of the airport's gravitational field.

The deep story: how the airport's cold-chain logistics corridor has spawned a silent economy stretching from Kempton Park to Nigel — one that handles perishable goods for half of Sub-Saharan Africa but is barely visible from street level.

Three supporting micro-stories: the informal cargo agents operating out of Tembisa who move small consignments across borders without ever touching the formal freight terminal; the emergence of a light-aviation maintenance cluster near Benoni's Rand Airport that feeds off overflow from OR Tambo; and the township-based courier ecosystem in Katlehong and Vosloorus that serves as the final-mile nervous system connecting e-commerce imports to township consumers.

The overlooked place: the Pomona Road logistics spine in Kempton Park — a seven-kilometre corridor of cold-storage facilities, freight yards, and fuel depots that almost no one outside the industry can name, yet which processes billions of rands in perishable imports each year.

The interesting statistic: OR Tambo International handles approximately 350,000 tonnes of air cargo annually — making it the single largest air cargo gateway on the African continent, and the engine around which much of Ekurhuleni's formal economy silently rotates.

Weekly Issue — 06 March 2026

The Invisible Port

How a single runway reshaped the economics of an entire region — and why most of its beneficiaries have never set foot inside a terminal.

1. Editorial
The Trucks That Move Before Sunrise

Sometime around four in the morning, on any weekday you care to choose, a particular kind of quiet descends on the N12 between Kempton Park and Boksburg. It lasts perhaps forty minutes. The casino billboards are still lit. The petrol stations glow. And then, almost without announcement, the trucks begin.

They move in loose convoys at first — refrigerated units mostly, their generators humming at a frequency you feel more than hear. They are heading for the perishables terminal at OR Tambo, or away from it, carrying Chilean grapes, Kenyan roses, Mozambican prawns, and pharmaceutical cold-chain cargo from Europe. By six o'clock the highway is full and the moment of quiet is gone, unremarked, as it is every morning.

I have been thinking about those trucks for some time now. Not about the trucks themselves, exactly, but about the question they raise without meaning to: when we say that Ekurhuleni is an 'industrial' region, what industry are we actually describing? The old answer — steel, springs, automotive parts, mining equipment — feels increasingly incomplete. Something else has been growing in the spaces between the old factories. Something faster, more networked, and considerably less visible.

The airport is the obvious starting point, but OR Tambo is not the whole story. It is more accurate to say that the airport is a kind of anchor — an economic magnet that has been pulling activities toward itself for thirty years, gradually reorganising the geography of the entire East Rand. Warehouses cluster in its shadow. Fuel infrastructure follows the cargo routes. Informal economies grow in the gaps between the formal logistics chains, filling the niches the large operators cannot or will not serve.

What makes this interesting — genuinely interesting, not simply impressive — is that almost none of it appears on any map most residents of the region would recognise. The Pomona Road cold-chain corridor is not on the tourism brochures. The small-consignment cargo agents working out of Tembisa are not in the Chamber of Commerce directory. The final-mile courier networks threading through Katlehong and Vosloorus are invisible to anyone who does not depend on them. And yet together they constitute something that might reasonably be described as the most consequential economic infrastructure in inland South Africa.

This issue is an attempt to draw that map.

2. The Long Essay
Cargo, Cold Chain, and the Continent's Hidden Port
Section I — The Observation

Drive east from OR Tambo along the R21 and then north on Pomona Road and you will pass, in quick succession, a cold-storage facility that holds cut flowers for seventeen African markets, a bonded warehouse handling dutiable electronics from the Far East, a temperature-controlled pharmaceutical logistics hub, a fuel depot that services both aircraft and the heavy trucks that feed the airport's appetite, and several unmarked buildings whose function you would have to ask about three times before anyone offered an answer. This is not a street. It is a supply chain given physical form.

The scale is difficult to convey without numbers, so here is one: the perishables terminal at OR Tambo is the largest of its kind in Africa. It handles, in a single year, more fresh produce by volume than the port of Durban. It does this invisibly, without ceremony, because the produce moves at night and the terminal itself is shielded from public view by the airport's perimeter. Most people who live within ten kilometres of it have never seen it.

The airport is not a building. It is a gravitational field — and Ekurhuleni is the economy that has grown in its orbit.
Section II — The Hidden System

To understand the airport economy, you have to think about it the way an ecologist thinks about a river delta — as a system that creates niches, and niches that attract specialised life. OR Tambo's cargo operation does not simply handle freight. It generates demand: for warehousing, for cold-chain logistics, for customs brokerage, for fuel, for security services, for light engineering and maintenance, and for the informal labour markets that fill the gaps in all of these formal industries.

The formal layer of this system is relatively well documented. Companies like DHL, Kuehne+Nagel, and Expeditors operate large facilities in the Kempton Park and Boksburg industrial zones. South African Airways Cargo — battered but persistent — maintains its own infrastructure at the terminal. These are the visible capstones of the system.

But the system's real complexity lives a layer down, in the mid-tier and informal operators who handle the freight that the majors do not want, cannot reach, or do not know exists. Consider the small-consignment cargo agents. These are individuals and small firms — many operating from modest premises in Tembisa, Daveyton, and along the Springs Road corridor — who specialise in consolidating small cross-border consignments: a consignment of mobile phone accessories from Guangzhou, a shipment of second-hand clothing from the UK, a parcel of medicinal plants from Zimbabwe. They aggregate these small loads, navigate the customs paperwork, and deliver to township traders and small retailers across Gauteng. They are, in effect, running a parallel import logistics system — one that is nimbler, cheaper, and more attuned to the actual demand patterns of township economies than any formal freight forwarder could be.

Then there is the final-mile problem. Large logistics companies are structurally unable to deliver profitably to dense, informal urban areas. The streets are narrow, the addresses unreliable, the security requirements expensive. Into this gap has flowed a fleet of local couriers — bakkie-based, motorbike-based, sometimes simply foot-based — operating in Katlehong, Vosloorus, and Thokoza. These networks have been amplified dramatically by the growth of e-commerce and cross-border online retail. Every parcel of clothing ordered from Chinese e-commerce platforms that arrives via OR Tambo eventually needs someone to carry it to a door in the township. That someone is almost never from a company you have heard of.

Section III — The Broader Insight

The airport economy of Ekurhuleni points toward something that urban economists have been circling for years without quite naming it: the emergence of what you might call a logistics civilisation. In the twentieth century, manufacturing was the organising principle of industrial cities. Cities like Ekurhuleni existed to make things. In the twenty-first century, the organising principle is increasingly movement — the ability to get things from where they are made to where they are needed, at speed, at scale, and at the right temperature.

This is not a story unique to South Africa. Rotterdam, Dubai, Memphis, Singapore — these cities are all, in different ways, logistics civilisations built around the movement of goods. What is unusual about Ekurhuleni is that it is attempting this transition while simultaneously managing the decline of its manufacturing base and the pressures of a township economy that has been systematically underinvested for a century. The logistics economy offers something valuable: it generates employment that does not require a large capital base to enter, it creates demand for services at multiple levels of skill and formality, and it connects local actors to global trade flows in ways that manufacturing no longer reliably does.

The risk, of course, is that the benefits of logistics civilisation concentrate at the top. Warehouses require land and capital, not labour. Automated sorting systems reduce headcount. The question for Ekurhuleni — the genuinely difficult question — is whether the informal and mid-tier layers of the airport economy can be nurtured rather than displaced as the formal sector grows.

Section IV — The Antifragile Angle

Here is the thing about the informal logistics networks of the East Rand that most formal analysis misses: they are not fragile. They are, in the precise sense Nassim Taleb intended, antifragile. They benefit from the shocks that damage the formal system.

When the rand fell sharply in 2015, formal importers contracted. Informal cargo agents expanded, because the demand for cheaper, consolidated small consignments increased. When COVID-19 disrupted formal retail supply chains in 2020, the township courier networks grew, because people who had never ordered online before suddenly needed goods delivered to their doors. When fuel prices spiked and large freight companies restructured their route networks, the gap was filled by smaller operators who could improvise, combine loads, and find efficiencies the big firms could not.

This pattern — formal system contracts, informal network expands — has repeated itself often enough that it is no longer sensible to describe the informal logistics economy as a temporary or residual phenomenon. It is a permanent feature of the airport economy, and arguably its most dynamic component. The city that learns to formalise the best parts of it — to give the small cargo agents access to bonded warehousing, to give the township couriers access to digital logistics infrastructure — will have found something genuinely valuable. A system that grows stronger under pressure is not a problem to be solved. It is an asset to be cultivated.

3. The Hidden Economy
Three Operations Nobody Is Watching
1. The Consolidators
Tembisa / Kempton Park Border Zone

Along the industrial edges of Tembisa, a cluster of small logistics agents — operating from converted garages and modest office blocks — specialise in consolidated airfreight. They receive goods on behalf of multiple small buyers, bundle the consignments to reduce per-kilo airfreight costs, and manage the customs clearance collectively. A single agent might handle twenty separate small traders' imports in one shipment, taking a margin on the consolidation. The economics work because the formal freight minimum charges make small imports prohibitively expensive for individual township traders; the consolidators effectively manufacture economies of scale that the formal system assumes you already have.

2. The Cold-Chain Couriers
Boksburg / Germiston Corridor

Between the large cold-storage facilities of the Pomona Road corridor and the butcheries, pharmacies, and fresh-food retailers of the East Rand townships, there is a gap that temperature-controlled delivery trucks cannot economically fill. Into it have moved a network of insulated-bakkie operators who purchase surplus or near-expiry cold-chain goods at the back doors of large distributors and deliver them, same-day, to township retailers at prices that undercut formal wholesale. They do not advertise. They operate on trust relationships built over years. Their margins are thin but their volume is consistent, and their knowledge of who needs what, when, and where is a form of market intelligence that no data company has yet found a way to replicate.

3. The Airport-Adjacent Mechanics
Benoni / Boksburg Light Industrial Zone

Rand Airport in Benoni is a minor aerodrome — light aircraft, flight schools, private charters. But its proximity to OR Tambo has created an unexpected secondary economy: a cluster of small aircraft maintenance workshops that handle the overflow maintenance, avionics checks, and parts fabrication that the major carriers' contracted MRO facilities cannot absorb quickly or cheaply enough. These workshops operate in the grey zone between full certification and informal practice, employing ex-ATNS and ex-SAA technicians who left the formal sector during the industry's turbulence. They charge a fraction of the certified rates and turn work around in days rather than weeks. The aviation industry knows they exist. It officially disapproves. It quietly relies on them.

4. The Map of the Week
Pomona Road, Kempton Park
26°6′22″S 28°14′11″E

Seven kilometres. That is the length of the Pomona Road logistics corridor running north from the R21 interchange in Kempton Park. In those seven kilometres you will find, depending on what you are looking for: cold-storage facilities handling cut flowers, fresh produce, and pharmaceuticals; bonded warehouses processing dutiable goods under customs lock; fuel depots supplying both aviation kerosene and road-freight diesel; and a succession of freight forwarding offices whose business cards are printed in English, Mandarin, and Portuguese.

What you will not find, conspicuously, is any signage that explains what any of it is for. The corridor is designed to be legible only to those who already know it. There are no visitor attractions, no retail frontage, no cafes. It is pure function — a piece of city that exists entirely in service of a supply chain. To walk it at dawn, when the refrigerated trucks are loading and the forklift operators are working in the cold-storage bays, is to understand that Ekurhuleni has a port. It is just not on the coast.

5. The East Rand Number
350,000 t Annual air cargo tonnage through OR Tambo International Airport

OR Tambo handles approximately 350,000 tonnes of air cargo per year, making it the largest air cargo gateway on the African continent by volume. To put this in perspective: that is roughly equivalent to the combined air cargo throughput of Lagos, Nairobi, and Cairo. It moves through a city that most South Africans think of primarily as a place to pass through on the way somewhere else. The cargo does not pass through. It arrives, and it stays, and it feeds an economy whose true size is not captured by any single statistic.

6. Closing Reflection
What the Runway Points Toward

There is a version of Ekurhuleni's future that is easy to miss because it does not announce itself. It does not arrive with a ribbon-cutting or a mayoral press release. It arrives in the form of a refrigerated truck at three in the morning, or a small cargo agent in Tembisa finding a cheaper route through Doha, or a bakkie driver in Vosloorus who has figured out that pharmaceutical deliveries pay better than groceries.

The airport economy is not a sector. It is a logic — a way of thinking about value that is organised around connection rather than production, around movement rather than manufacture. That logic is spreading outward from OR Tambo like a slow tide, reaching further each year into the township economies that surround the formal logistics belt. The question that matters for the next decade is not whether Ekurhuleni can compete globally. It already does. The question is whether the people who have built its informal logistics infrastructure from scratch will be among the beneficiaries of what comes next — or merely its unseen foundation.
