EKURHULENIUNDERGROUND

The hidden systems of the East Rand

Theme Analysis — Issue 2

The Bale Trade

Why this theme matters for the long-term economic story of Ekurhuleni

The theme of the Bale Trade is not a story about secondhand clothing. It is a story about a complete import-to-retail system — one that sources goods from charity collection points in the United Kingdom, Canada, and the United States, moves them through international freight networks to South African ports, distributes them through a tiered sorting and grading economy across the East Rand's townships, and delivers them to consumers at price points that no formal clothing retailer has ever managed to match. The bale is the unit of commerce that makes this system visible. The system itself is far older, larger, and more sophisticated than the bale suggests.

The deep story: how the compressed bale of mixed secondhand clothing — arriving at Ekurhuleni's informal wholesale markets in Daveyton, Katlehong, and Vosloorus from importers operating out of Johannesburg and Durban — has become the raw material of a tiered sorting, grading, and retail economy that employs tens of thousands of people across the East Rand and constitutes the primary clothing market for a significant portion of the region's population. This is not informal retail at the margins of a formal clothing economy. In the townships this publication covers, the bale trade is the clothing economy.

Three supporting micro-stories: the bale importers of Johannesburg's CBD and Benoni industrial fringe who source, clear, and distribute compressed bales to township resellers across the East Rand; the sorting and grading operations that operate from converted warehouses and open yards in Katlehong and Tsakane, where the mixed contents of each bale are inspected, categorised, and priced by operators whose product knowledge rivals any formal retail buyer's; and the cross-border dimension — the buyers from Zimbabwe, Mozambique, and Zambia who visit the East Rand's bale markets regularly, making the region an informal clothing export hub for southern Africa.

The overlooked place: the Katlehong bale market precinct along the Elsburg Road corridor — a dense cluster of open-air and covered bale sorting operations, resale stalls, and wholesale yards that processes more clothing volume on a busy Saturday than many formal shopping centre precincts, and that appears in no commercial property register and no retail industry report.

The interesting statistic: South Africa imports an estimated 100,000 tonnes of used clothing annually — making it one of the largest secondhand clothing import markets in the world — and a disproportionate share of this flows through the informal distribution networks of the Gauteng township economy, including the East Rand's extensive bale trading corridor.

Weekly Issue — 30 January 2026

The Second Life Economy

How a compressed bale of used clothing from a charity shop in Birmingham becomes the most consequential unit of commerce in Katlehong — and the hidden import, sorting, and retail system that makes the journey possible.

1. Editorial
The Saturday Morning of Everything

By seven on a Saturday morning, the Katlehong bale market is already loud. Not aggressively loud — not the amplified commercial noise of a formal retail environment — but dense with a particular kind of productive sound: the slap of a bale being cut open, the low conversation of two traders negotiating a price, the rustle of clothing being lifted and assessed and stacked into piles whose categories are visible only to the people doing the sorting. The market occupies a strip of ground along Elsburg Road that nobody seems to have formally designated for commerce. The stalls are temporary but the traders are permanent. Most of them have been here for years. Some of them have been here for decades.

I have been coming to this market, or markets like it, for some time now. What draws me is not the merchandise — though the merchandise, examined carefully, is its own kind of economic anthropology, each garment a trace of a previous life in another country and another climate. What draws me is the intelligence of the operation. The woman who buys a bale for R800 and has it sorted and repriced into sellable stock within ninety minutes has not simply bought secondhand clothes. She has performed an act of arbitrage: converting an undifferentiated compressed block of mixed value into a graded, priced, retail-ready product. The knowledge that makes this possible — the ability to assess fabric quality, brand recognition, seasonal suitability, and local demand preference while processing fifty garments in ten minutes — is not something that can be learned from a textbook. It is accumulated through years of market practice. It is, in the precise sense that economists mean, human capital.

The bale trade of the East Rand is one of those economic systems that is so visible on the ground and so invisible in the data that it takes a deliberate act of attention to see it for what it is. It is not, as it is sometimes described, a poverty economy — a residual market for people who cannot afford formal retail. It is a complete import-to-retail system, with its own supply chains, its own grading standards, its own credit relationships, and its own price discovery mechanisms. It connects a charity donation point in Manchester or Toronto to a stall on Elsburg Road through a chain of intermediaries that spans two continents, three modes of transport, and at least four distinct commercial layers. That the chain is informal does not make it simple. It makes it, in several important ways, more sophisticated than the formal clothing retail system it coexists with.

This issue is an attempt to trace the chain from its origins to its endpoints — and to ask what it reveals about the East Rand's position in a global trade system that its residents participate in daily without anyone having thought to describe that participation as trade.

2. The Long Essay
From the Charity Bin to the Saturday Stall
Section I — The Observation

The bale arrives at a loading yard in Benoni as a compressed block approximately the size of a washing machine, wrapped in thick plastic sheeting and bound with steel banding. It weighs between 45 and 55 kilograms. Its contents are unknown — or rather, they are known only in aggregate terms: it is a Grade A mixed bale of ladies' clothing, or a Grade B mixed bale of children's wear, sourced from a collection depot in the United Kingdom and processed through a sorting facility in Durban. The grade indicates the general quality range of the contents; it does not specify individual items. The trader who buys the bale is, in this sense, making a probabilistic bet — betting that the aggregate value of the garments inside, once sorted and priced for the local market, will exceed the price she paid for the bale plus her labour costs. She has made this bet before. She is usually right.

The bale economy operates at city scale across the East Rand's townships, but it is not evenly distributed. Certain corridors have emerged as nodes: the Elsburg Road strip in Katlehong, the market precincts adjacent to the main taxi ranks in Vosloorus and Daveyton, the converted warehouse yards on the Tsakane industrial fringe. These nodes are not accidents. They have emerged at the points where distribution logistics, consumer footfall, and available physical space have aligned — which, in the township context, usually means proximity to a major taxi rank and access to a main road for delivery vehicles. The same spatial logic that organises formal retail has organised the bale economy. It has simply done so without a developer's brief or a municipal zoning decision.

The woman who sorts a bale in ninety minutes is not running a charity shop. She is running an import business, a quality control operation, and a retail pricing system simultaneously — without any of these functions being named.
Section II — The Hidden System

The bale trade of the East Rand operates through four distinct layers, each performing a specialised function and each connected to the others through relationships of credit, trust, and accumulated market knowledge.

The first layer is the importer. Bale importers — operating from warehouses and forwarding offices concentrated in Johannesburg's industrial suburbs and the Benoni-Boksburg corridor — source compressed bales from international consolidators in the United Kingdom, Canada, the United States, and increasingly from East African collection points. They handle the customs clearance, the freight forwarding, and the initial grading that determines how the bale is classified and priced for the domestic market. Several importers in the East Rand operate at significant scale: moving hundreds of bales per week to township resellers across Gauteng, with pricing structures and credit terms that function, in all essential respects, like a wholesale distribution business. They are simply wholesale distribution businesses that handle clothing measured by the compressed bale rather than by the individual garment.

The second layer is the primary reseller — the trader who buys bales directly from the importer, cuts them open, sorts and grades the contents, and either retails directly at a stall or supplies secondary resellers at a margin. The sorting operation is where the system's most distinctive knowledge concentrates. Experienced sorters can assess a garment's grade, brand value, and local marketability in seconds. They build piles instinctively: premium items that will carry a significant markup, branded goods that carry a brand premium even in the secondhand market, items suitable for the township's colder winter months stacked separately from summer-weight stock, garments that are too worn or damaged for retail sorted into a rag pile that has its own downstream market in industrial cleaning and stuffing materials. Nothing is wasted. Every sub-category of the bale's contents has a buyer.

The third layer is the secondary and tertiary retail network — the stalls, the street traders, the backyard sellers, and the growing category of social media resellers who photograph premium bale finds and sell through WhatsApp and Facebook Marketplace to customers they have never met in person. This layer is where the bale economy has expanded most rapidly in the past five years, as smartphone penetration has made it possible to reach buyers beyond the immediate geographic catchment of the physical stall. A trader in Katlehong can now sell to a buyer in Tsakane without either party leaving their home, using a logistics network of taxi freight and door-to-door delivery that has grown up in parallel with the social media retail layer.

The fourth layer — least visible and in some ways most consequential — is the cross-border export dimension. The East Rand's bale markets attract buyers from Zimbabwe, Mozambique, Zambia, and Malawi who travel to the region specifically to purchase sorted bale stock for resale in their home countries. The economics work because the differential between the South African informal market price and the Harare or Maputo retail price is large enough to make the cross-border journey worthwhile. These buyers often have long-standing relationships with specific East Rand sorters and resellers — relationships built over years of repeat purchasing — and their buying trips constitute a form of informal export that appears in no trade statistics. The East Rand, in this light, is not just a domestic clothing market. It is a regional clothing distribution hub.

Section III — The Broader Insight

What the bale trade reveals about Ekurhuleni's economic position is something that formal trade analysis consistently misses: the region's township economies are not simply consumers of imported goods. They are, in many categories, the distributors. The goods arrive — whether as compressed clothing bales, as electronics from Chinese importers, as agricultural produce from the fresh produce market — and the township economy sorts, grades, prices, and distributes them through a retail infrastructure that reaches deeper into residential areas, operates at lower margins, and serves customers at more convenient times than any formal retailer has managed to replicate. This is not informal retail as a substitute for formal retail. It is an alternative retail architecture that performs the same functions more efficiently for the specific demand characteristics of the township market.

The bale trade is particularly instructive because it makes the import-to-retail chain visible in a way that most informal trade does not. Each bale is a discrete unit — a legible economic event — that enters the system at a known price and exits as retail-ready stock at a known margin. The aggregate economics of the system, if anyone thought to measure them carefully, would reveal a clothing market of considerable scale operating entirely outside the formal retail data that the industry's analysts use to understand consumer demand. The fashion brands whose garments circulate through the bale economy — often brands available new in formal retail — have no visibility into this channel. Their products are reaching township consumers through a distribution system they have never designed for and cannot influence.

Section IV — The Antifragile Angle

The bale trade of the East Rand has a structural relationship to economic volatility that makes it one of the most consistently resilient informal economies in the region. When the rand depreciates sharply — as it did in 2015, 2020, and at several points since — the cost of new clothing imported through formal retail channels rises immediately and substantially. The bale trade, whose pricing is denominated in rand and whose merchandise is already-imported, does not face the same pass-through effect. In rand terms, a sorted bale's contents are priced against local market purchasing power rather than against import costs. The depreciation that squeezes formal clothing retailers expands the bale economy's competitive advantage.

The same logic applies to unemployment shocks. When formal employment contracts and household disposable income falls, the volume of consumers seeking bale-economy price points increases. When load-shedding disrupted formal retail through 2023 and 2024 — forcing shopping centres to close during outage windows and driving consumers toward markets that did not depend on Eskom — the open-air bale stalls operated uninterrupted. The outdoor Saturday market in Katlehong does not have an electricity bill. It does not have a generator requirement. It is, by design, off-grid. Each crisis that tightens the formal economy sends another wave of consumers toward the bale stall. The trader who has been there for twenty years has seen this pattern enough times that she no longer needs the economist to explain it. She simply stocks up before the school holidays and waits for the Saturday crowd.

3. The Hidden Economy
Three Operations Nobody Is Watching
1. The Bale Importers
Benoni Industrial Fringe / Boksburg North

From warehouses in the Benoni-Boksburg industrial corridor, a cluster of bale importers operates what are, in functional terms, clothing wholesale distribution businesses of significant scale. They source compressed bales from consolidators in the UK, Canada, and the US — typically through brokers who aggregate donations from multiple charity networks — arrange shipping to Durban, handle customs clearance under the tariff codes that apply to secondhand clothing, and distribute to township resellers across Gauteng. Their pricing structures offer volume discounts and credit terms that have been refined through years of relationship-building with their township client base. Several operate their own delivery fleet. None of this appears in any clothing industry report, because the importers are classified, if they appear in any formal data at all, as general goods traders rather than clothing wholesalers. The category obscures the scale.

2. The Premium Sorters
Katlehong / Tsakane Sorting Yards

Within the bale trade ecosystem, a small number of operators have built reputations specifically for their ability to extract premium value from mixed bales through superior sorting. These are not simply faster or more experienced sorters. They have developed the product knowledge — brand recognition, fabric quality assessment, current demand patterns in their specific township market — that allows them to identify items within a bale whose resale value significantly exceeds what a less experienced sorter would price them at. Their reputation draws buyers willing to pay a premium specifically for their stock, and some have built waiting lists of cross-border buyers who commission specific sorts — winter men's wear only, branded sportswear only — and pay above-market prices for the curation. The sort, in their hands, has become a product. The knowledge is the business.

3. The Cross-Border Buyers
Daveyton / Vosloorus Wholesale Markets

On the first weekend of each month — a rhythm that several Katlehong and Daveyton bale traders can confirm without checking a calendar — buyers arrive from Zimbabwe and Mozambique. They travel by long-distance bus, occasionally by shared taxi, always with large empty holdall bags and a specific shopping list compiled over weeks of WhatsApp communication with the township traders they have dealt with for years. They buy sorted stock rather than unsorted bales — they need certainty of quality for a market that is a border crossing away from the sorting yard — paying prices that reflect both the quality premium and the distribution margin they need to make the journey worthwhile. They leave with bags that will not be checked under formal trade documentation. They return the following month. The East Rand clothing market, in this transaction, extends to Harare and Maputo. It has done so, quietly, for two decades.

4. The Map of the Week
The Elsburg Road Bale Corridor, Katlehong
26°21′S  28°9′E

Approximately one kilometre. That is the active length of the Elsburg Road bale trading corridor in Katlehong — a stretch of road-adjacent trading space where, on any Saturday from dawn to mid-afternoon, the East Rand's secondhand clothing economy is most visibly concentrated. In that kilometre you will find: open-air sorting operations where bales are being cut and processed in real time; covered stalls where yesterday's sort has been priced and racked for retail; wholesale sections where larger operators deal in sorted stock to smaller traders rather than to end consumers; and the peripheral activities that any significant market generates — food vendors, airtime sellers, the transport logistics of traders arriving and departing with stock in the boots of shared taxis.

What you will not find is any commercial signage, any formal lease agreement, or any record in the municipal commercial register. The corridor operates on an informal licensing system — traders pay a daily pitch fee to a market committee that allocates space, mediates disputes, and maintains the minimal infrastructure the market requires. The committee has no legal standing. The market has no formal existence. On the Saturday morning it processes more clothing than most formal retail stores manage in a week.

5. The East Rand Number
100,000 t Estimated annual volume of used clothing imported into South Africa — making it one of the world's largest secondhand clothing import markets

South Africa imports an estimated 100,000 tonnes of used clothing annually, placing it among the largest secondhand clothing importers globally by volume. The Gauteng township economy — and specifically the East Rand corridor connecting Katlehong, Vosloorus, Tsakane, and Daveyton — absorbs a significant share of this through the informal bale distribution networks that have developed over three decades of trade. The formal clothing retail industry, which tracks market share through store turnover and consumer survey data, has no visibility into this channel. The data gap is not a measurement failure. It is the consequence of an industry analysing its own distribution architecture and concluding that the majority of the market it operates in does not exist.

6. Closing Reflection
The Global Trade That Looks Like a Stall

There is something instructive about the bale economy's invisibility — not just to formal data systems, but to the casual observer who passes Elsburg Road on a Saturday without looking closely. What appears to be a secondhand market is, on examination, a node in a global trade network: the downstream end of a supply chain that begins with a donation in Birmingham and ends with a garment on a body in Katlehong, having passed through a UK sorting facility, a Durban port, a Benoni importer, a Katlehong sorting yard, and a Saturday stall in the space of perhaps six weeks. The system that performs this supply chain function — without a single formal procurement contract, without a single official trade designation, without appearing in any industry association's membership list — is one of the most complete informal trading systems on the continent. It is also, measured against its own objectives, one of the most efficient. The woman at the stall knows more about her customers' preferences, size distributions, and price sensitivity than any formal retailer's analytics team. She has been measuring these things, by hand, for twenty years.
