EKURHULENIUNDERGROUND

The hidden systems of the East Rand

Inaugural Issue  |  23 January 2026
Theme Analysis — Issue 1

The Fresh Food System

Why this theme matters for the long-term economic story of Ekurhuleni

The theme of the Fresh Food System is not a story about vegetables. It is a story about logistics — about how a metropolitan population of 3.5 million people, the majority of whom live in township residential areas underserved by formal retail, is fed every day by a distributed supply chain that no supermarket designed, no municipality manages, and no economist has adequately measured.

The deep story: how the Johannesburg Fresh Produce Market at City Deep — the largest fresh produce market in sub-Saharan Africa — functions as the hidden alimentary root of Ekurhuleni's township economy, its produce flowing outward through a tiered network of bulk bakkie traders, neighbourhood produce wholesalers, and street vendors that reaches further, faster, and more cheaply into residential streets than any formal retail supply chain has ever managed.

Three supporting micro-stories: the pre-dawn bakkie traders who buy at City Deep before four in the morning and supply produce markets and spaza shops across Katlehong, Vosloorus, and Tsakane before the formal retail sector has opened its doors; the neighbourhood produce points — fixed stalls or regular pavement pitches — that serve as the primary fresh food source for households within walking distance and extend informal credit to regular customers across the weekly income cycle; and the township catering economy of Duduza and Daveyton, where home-based food producers buy daily from local produce traders to supply construction sites, taxi ranks, and informal workplaces with cooked food that formal catering cannot reach at the price point the market demands.

The overlooked place: the Vosloorus fresh produce strip along Ntuli Street — a kilometre of pavement trading that begins before dawn and constitutes the primary food market for a residential area of more than 100,000 people, without a single formal lease agreement, municipal market licence, or entry in any retail industry database.

The interesting statistic: the Johannesburg Fresh Produce Market at City Deep processes more than 650,000 tonnes of fresh produce annually — making it the largest fresh produce market on the African continent — and the majority of its throughput exits through informal distribution channels that reach township consumers directly, bypassing the formal retail supply chain entirely.

Weekly Issue — 23 January 2026

The City That Feeds Itself

How Ekurhuleni's township economies built a fresh food distribution system that feeds millions daily — and why the formal retail sector has spent thirty years failing to understand what it is competing with.

1. Editorial
An Introduction, and a Question

This is the first issue of Ekurhuleni Underground. Before we get into the story, a few words about what this publication is for — and what it is not for.

Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality is, by most industrial measures, one of the most consequential economic zones on the African continent. It handles more air cargo than any other airport on the continent. Its mine dumps contain the largest concentration of reclaimable gold tailings on earth. Its logistics corridors connect the inland economy of southern Africa to its ports. Its township economies — Katlehong, Vosloorus, Tembisa, Daveyton, Tsakane — process goods, capital, and labour at a scale that dwarfs many provincial cities. And yet Ekurhuleni is, in the public conversation about South Africa's economy, almost entirely absent. It is the city that most South Africans drive through without thinking about. The city whose importance is assumed and whose systems are never explained.

This publication exists to change that. Not through news reporting or political commentary. Not through the complaint genre that dominates most local coverage. But through the patient, deliberate work of economic explanation — of asking: what is actually happening here, and why? Each week, this publication will focus on one hidden system: a supply chain that nobody has mapped, an informal economy that nobody has counted, an infrastructure dependency that nobody has named. The reader we are writing for is anyone who suspects that their city is more consequential than the conversation about it suggests.

We begin with food. Not because food is the most dramatic system in Ekurhuleni, but because it is the most immediate. Three times a day, millions of people in this municipality eat. The question of where that food comes from — how it moves from farm to table across the specific geography of the East Rand, who handles it, who prices it, who extends credit so that a household can eat on Thursday before Friday's wage arrives — is a question with a surprisingly complex answer. An answer that the formal economy has never fully understood, and that the informal economy has been providing, efficiently and at scale, for decades without acknowledgement.

2. The Long Essay
The Market That Starts at Three
Section I — The Observation

The Johannesburg Fresh Produce Market at City Deep never fully closes. It comes closest to still between midnight and two in the morning, when the last trucks of the previous day's buying have left and the first trucks of the new day have not yet arrived. But by three o'clock, the activity begins again: the wholesale buyers moving through the market's covered halls under fluorescent light, examining tomatoes, negotiating on spinach, loading bakkies with boxes of onions and potatoes and cabbages that will be on a pavement in Katlehong before the sun is up. These are not the large formal buyers — the supermarket procurement teams and restaurant supply chains that dominate the market's daytime hours. These are the pre-dawn traders, and they operate according to a logic that is entirely their own.

The pre-dawn trader at City Deep is typically a small operator: a bakkie, a float of buying capital — sometimes borrowed, often accumulated across years of small margins — and a precise knowledge of what the produce points and spaza shops on a defined route will need that morning. He does not use a procurement system. He uses his memory and his phone and the relationships he has built with specific produce sellers in the market's A and B halls over years of pre-dawn trade. He buys in quantities calibrated to the capacity of his vehicle and the demand of his route. He prices his load mentally as he buys, estimating the margin he will make by the time he has sold out, which will be before nine o'clock.

The bakkie trader does not compete with the supermarket. He serves a market the supermarket structurally cannot reach — at the time it is needed, at the price the household can pay, on the credit terms that make the transaction possible at all.
Section II — The Hidden System

The informal fresh food system of the East Rand operates across three distinct tiers, each performing a function that the tier above and below it cannot replicate, and each connected to the others through relationships of credit, information, and accumulated territorial knowledge.

The first tier is the bulk bakkie trader — the pre-dawn City Deep buyer who supplies the East Rand's township produce points and larger spaza shops with daily stock. These operators are the arterial layer of the system: they move volume, they set the daily wholesale price in the township market, and they carry the risk of the perishable commodity they have purchased on capital they cannot afford to lose. The route a bulk bakkie trader maintains is not simply a delivery schedule. It is a set of credit relationships, a demand intelligence network, and a logistics operation calibrated to the precise perishability of whatever he is carrying on a given day. A tomato has a different tolerance for heat and delay than a bunch of spinach. The bakkie trader knows this in his hands, not in a spreadsheet.

The second tier is the neighbourhood produce point — the fixed pavement stall or regular pitch that serves as the primary fresh food retail outlet for the households within walking distance. These operators buy from the bulk bakkie traders, sometimes daily, and sell in the quantities that township purchasing power requires: a single onion, four tomatoes, a handful of spinach — quantities that no formal retailer stocks or prices, because the formal model assumes weekly shopping budgets rather than daily cash management. The produce point extends credit to regular customers with a reliability that would surprise any formal credit assessor: the woman who buys on account every day and settles on Friday knows, and is known by, the vendor in a way that makes the credit relationship self-enforcing. Default is social failure, not merely financial failure. The default rate, in consequence, is low.

The third tier is the value-added food economy — the home-based producers, street food vendors, and informal caterers who buy fresh produce from the neighbourhood produce points and convert it into cooked food for sale at taxi ranks, construction sites, factory gates, and school precincts. This tier is where the fresh food system articulates with the broader township labour economy: the construction worker who cannot carry a packed lunch and the taxi driver who eats three meals a day at the rank are both served by an informal catering network that depends entirely on the fresh food supply chain beneath it. The price of a plate of pap and vegetables at a Katlehong taxi rank is, in a direct and traceable way, a function of what a bakkie trader paid for spinach at City Deep at four that morning.

Section III — The Broader Insight

The fresh food system of the East Rand illustrates a principle that this publication will return to in different forms across many issues: informal economies are not primitive versions of formal economies. They are often more precisely calibrated to the actual demand characteristics of the markets they serve. The formal fresh food retail model — weekly shopping, fixed pricing, standardised pack sizes, credit extended through formal financial instruments — is designed for a consumer with a monthly income, a car, a freezer, and a predictable schedule. The informal produce point is designed for a consumer with a daily income, no car, a small refrigerator or no refrigerator, and a budget that is managed hour by hour rather than week by week. These are not two versions of the same system. They are two different systems, serving two different economic realities, and the informal one serves its reality considerably better.

What makes this significant for Ekurhuleni's long-term economic story is the question of what happens as the formal retail sector expands into township areas — as it has been attempting, with mixed success, for the past two decades. The standard assumption is that formal retail displaces informal: that the supermarket kills the spaza shop and the produce point. The evidence from the East Rand is more complicated. Formal retailers have captured a share of the weekly grocery shop. They have not displaced the daily fresh food system, because they cannot serve it on its own terms. The bakkie trader at four in the morning, the produce point with its single-onion pricing and its Friday-settlement credit, the home caterer who needs fresh spinach by seven — none of these actors are waiting to be replaced. They are filling structural gaps that the formal system is architecturally incapable of closing.

Section IV — The Antifragile Angle

The informal fresh food system of the East Rand has been stress-tested repeatedly over the past decade and has demonstrated, each time, the property that distinguishes truly resilient systems from merely robust ones: it does not just survive shocks. It adapts to them and in some cases benefits from them.

When fuel prices spiked in 2022, formal retail supply chains faced significant cost pressures. The bakkie trader's fuel cost also rose — but his route density meant that his cost-per-delivery-point remained lower than any formal distribution vehicle could achieve in the same territory. When load-shedding disrupted formal cold chain storage in 2023, fresh produce that could not be stored by formal retailers flowed into the informal market at reduced prices, temporarily expanding the informal system's supply and reducing its input costs. When the COVID-19 pandemic restricted movement in 2020 and closed formal retail for extended periods, the informal fresh food network — operating from pavements, exempt from many of the restrictions that closed indoor trading — became the primary food access point for millions of East Rand residents. Each crisis that compromised the formal system created a demand surge for the informal one.

This is not coincidence. It is structure. A system built on small operators, flexible capital, daily price adjustment, and deep local knowledge is a system that can reorganise itself faster than any formal supply chain can respond to a shock. The question it raises for Ekurhuleni — for the municipality that governs it, the planners who design its streets, the retailers who eye its market share — is not how to replace this system. It is how to acknowledge it clearly enough to stop accidentally damaging it: with by-law enforcement that removes pavement traders without understanding what they replace, with road designs that prioritise vehicle throughput over the pedestrian accessibility that the produce point depends on, with formal market development schemes that compete with rather than complement the network already in place.

3. The Hidden Economy
Three Operations Nobody Is Watching
1. The Pre-Dawn Buyers
City Deep → Katlehong / Vosloorus / Tsakane Routes

Between two-thirty and five in the morning, a fleet of bakkies and small trucks makes its way from the East Rand's townships to the Johannesburg Fresh Produce Market at City Deep, loads, and returns. The operators who run these routes have typically done so for years — some for decades — and their knowledge of the market's A and B halls is encyclopaedic: which sellers carry the best tomatoes in winter, which stone fruit agent will negotiate on the last boxes of a perishable line, which spinach variety travels best to the Vosloorus route without wilting before the morning's selling is done. They buy on cash, load fast, and are gone before the formal retail buyers arrive. By the time Ekurhuleni's formal economy opens for business, they have already completed a full day's distribution cycle. Their annual throughput, across the network of operators working East Rand routes, has never been estimated. It is large.

2. The Produce Credit Keepers
Vosloorus / Katlehong Neighbourhood Produce Points

Along the residential streets of Vosloorus and Katlehong, the operators of fixed produce points maintain what are, in effect, informal credit ledgers: notebooks or phone notes recording who has bought on account, how much they owe, and when they typically settle. The credit extended is small by formal standards — R20, R50, occasionally R100 — but it is precisely calibrated to the cash flow rhythms of the households the vendor serves: extended on Monday when wages have not yet arrived, settled on Friday or the twenty-fifth of the month when they have. The vendor knows which customers have a domestic worker income cycle and which have a construction wage cycle. She prices her credit extension accordingly. No interest is charged. The social relationship is the collateral. The default rate, across vendors who have been running these ledgers for years, is a fraction of what any formal microlender achieves.

3. The Township Caterers
Duduza / Daveyton Construction and Industrial Corridors

In the industrial and construction zones of Duduza and Daveyton, a network of home-based caterers supplies cooked food to workers who cannot bring their own meals and cannot access formal catering at the price point their wages allow. These caterers — operating from domestic kitchens, sometimes from repurposed garages — buy fresh produce from neighbourhood produce points daily, cook before five in the morning, and deliver to construction sites and industrial precincts by seven. Their menus are fixed and their prices are known in advance; the construction worker who eats from the same caterer every day pays a weekly rate negotiated informally and adjusted quarterly for produce price movements. The caterers have, in effect, built a subscription food service operating across a territory that no food delivery platform has yet reached profitably. They did not plan it that way. They simply solved the problem in front of them.

4. The Map of the Week
Ntuli Street Produce Strip, Vosloorus
26°20′S  28°8′E

Approximately one kilometre. That is the length of the active produce trading corridor along Ntuli Street in Vosloorus — a stretch of pavement-based fresh food trading that begins before dawn and winds down by mid-afternoon, serving the food needs of a surrounding residential population estimated at over 100,000 people. In that kilometre you will find: the fixed-pitch operators who have held the same position for a decade, with their umbrellas and their crates of onions and their well-thumbed credit notebooks; the mobile traders who fill the gaps between fixed pitches with whatever has come off the bakkie that morning at a price that undercuts the fixed stalls by enough to justify the lack of permanence; and at the corridor's two taxi rank intersections, the cooked food sellers whose produce comes from the very stalls twenty metres away, completing a supply chain loop so tight that the spinach on the plate at lunchtime was still on the City Deep floor at four that morning.

No municipal market authority administers this corridor. No lease agreement governs the pitches. The order that exists — and there is order: established operators are not displaced by newcomers without community sanction, pitch sizes are respected, the pavement is kept clear enough for pedestrian traffic — is maintained by the traders themselves through social norms built over years of daily proximity. It is, in the precise sense that economists mean, a self-governing commons. The municipality that learns to work with it rather than around it will have found something more durable than any formal market infrastructure it could build in its place.

5. The East Rand Number
650,000 t Annual fresh produce throughput at City Deep — the continent's largest fresh produce market, and the hidden alimentary root of Ekurhuleni's township food economy

The Johannesburg Fresh Produce Market at City Deep processes more than 650,000 tonnes of fresh produce annually — citrus, vegetables, stone fruit, potatoes, onions — making it the largest fresh produce market on the African continent by volume. A significant proportion of this throughput does not enter the formal retail supply chain at all. It exits through the pre-dawn informal buying network that supplies township produce points across the greater East Rand. The formal retail sector, which tracks consumer spending through loyalty card data and till roll analysis, has no visibility into this channel. The gap between what the market processes and what formal retail accounts for is not leakage. It is a distribution system. It simply operates at hours and in volumes that the formal data systems were not designed to see.

6. Closing Reflection
What This City Is

Every week, this publication will try to answer the same underlying question in a different register: what is Ekurhuleni, actually? Not what its official economic profiles claim it to be, not what the road signs suggest as you pass through it, but what it is in the operational sense — in the sense of what systems are running, what knowledge is being applied, what value is being created, and by whom. The answer, we suspect, is considerably more interesting than the standard account allows. The fresh food system is one piece of it. It is, in its way, a template for all the pieces that will follow: the informal system that performs a function the formal system cannot, at a scale the formal system does not acknowledge, with a resilience the formal system cannot replicate. Ekurhuleni is full of these systems. This publication exists to find them. We are glad you are here for the first one.

— Ekurhuleni Underground, 23 January 2026
